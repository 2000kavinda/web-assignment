Though count of people who affect from poverty was reduced by it’s half during 1990 – 2015 period, still there are 736 millions of people who don’t have even food, clean water and sanitary facilities.Countries like India and China have decreased the poverty by their faster development. But it’s progress is not equally spread around the world. SDGs are a bold commitment to end what we started and to end poverty by 2030 in all its forms and dimensions. This includes increasing basic resources and services that are most vulnerable, and assisting communities affected by conflict and climate-related disasters.







The number of people suffering from malnutrition has dropped by almost half in the last two decades due to rapid economic growth and increasing agricultural productivity. Central and East Asia, Latin America and the Caribbean have all made great strides in eradicating hunger. It is estimated that by 2017, 821 million people will be suffering from chronic malnutrition, often as a direct result of environmental degradation, drought and loss of biodiversity. SDGs aims to end all forms of hunger and malnutrition by 2030, ensuring that all people - especially children - have access to adequate and nutritious food throughout the year. This includes sustainable agricultural promotion, assistance to small farmers and a similar approach to land, technology and markets. International cooperation is also needed to ensure investment in infrastructure and technology to increase agricultural productivity.






Life expectancy has increased dramatically; Infant and maternal mortality rates have dropped, and we've been living with HIV. And malaria deaths have been halved. Good health is essential for sustainable development and the 2030 Agenda reflects the complexity and interrelationship between the two. It addresses growing economic and social inequalities, rapid urbanization, threats to climate and the environment, the continuing burden of HIV and other infectious diseases, and emerging challenges such as noncommunicable diseases. Universal health coverage is essential to achieving SDG 3, ending poverty and reducing inequality. Progress has been unequal between and within countries. There is a gap of 31 years between the countries with the shortest and longest life expectancy.







Since 2000, there has been tremendous progress in achieving the goal of universal primary education. The total enrollment rate in developing regions reached 91 percent in 2015, and the number of out-of-school children worldwide has dropped by almost half. There has also been a huge increase in literacy rates, with more girls going to school than ever before. These are all outstanding successes. Progress has also intensified in some developing regions due to high levels of poverty, armed conflict and other emergencies. In West Asia and North Africa, the number of out-of-school children has risen due to the ongoing armed conflict. The goal is to ensure that all girls and boys complete free primary and secondary schooling by 2030. It also aims to provide equal access to affordable vocational training, eliminate gender and wealth inequality, and universal access to quality higher education.






The aim is end all discrimination against women and girls. Empowerment of women and girls has been shown to contribute to economic growth and development. But while there are more women in the labor market than ever before, there are still huge inequalities in some regions, with women systematically being denied the same service rights as men. Sexual violence and exploitation, unpaid care and unequal distribution of domestic service and discrimination in government offices all remain major obstacles. Climate change and disaster are having a disproportionate impact on women and children, as are conflicts and migration. It is important that women have equal rights to land and property, sexual and reproductive health and technology and the Internet.







Water scarcity affects more than 40 percent of the population, and is alarming as temperatures rise. It is predicted that by 2050, at least one in four people will experience repeated water shortages.By 2030, there is a need to invest in adequate infrastructure for safe and affordable drinking water, to provide sanitation and to encourage sanitation. Protecting and restoring water-based ecosystems is essential.








Between 2000 and 2016, the number of people without electricity increased from 78 to 87 percent, and the number without electricity was just under a billion. But as the population continues to grow, so will the demand for cheap energy, and a fossil fuel-dependent economy will make a drastic difference in our climate. Achieving SDG 7 by 2030 is critical to investing in solar, wind and thermal power, improving energy productivity and ensuring energy for all.








In developing countries, the middle class now accounts for more than 34 percent of total employment - a figure that tripled between 1991 and 2015. However, as the global economy continues to recover, we see slower growth, wider Inequalities and the lack of enough jobs to keep up with the growing workforce. SDGs promote sustainable economic growth, high productivity and technological innovation. Encouraging entrepreneurship and job creation are key to this and are effective measures to eradicate forced labor, slavery and human trafficking. The goal is to achieve full and productive employment and good employment for all women and men by 2030.







Investing in infrastructure and innovation are crucial drivers of economic growth and development. With more than half of the world's population now living in cities, mass transportation and renewable energy, as well as the growth of new industries and information and communication technologies, are becoming more and more important. Technological progress is also key. Promoting sustainable industries and investing in scientific research and innovation are important ways to facilitate sustainable development.








Income inequality has increased almost everywhere in recent decades, but at different speeds. It is the lowest in Europe and the highest in the Middle East.These widening disparities require good policies to empower low-income earners, and promote economic inclusion of all, regardless of gender, race or ethnicity. These include improving the regulation and oversight of financial markets and institutions, developing aid to areas with high demand, and encouraging foreign direct investment.








By 2050, two-thirds of the world's population - 6.5 billion people - will be urban. The rapid growth of cities - a result of rising population and migration - has led to the boom, especially in the developing world, and slums are becoming an increasingly important part of urban life. Urban sustainability is about creating professional and business opportunities, building secure and affordable housing, and building resilient societies and economies. It includes investing in public transportation, creating green public spaces, and improving urban planning and management through participatory and inclusive routes.







Agriculture is the largest user of water worldwide, and irrigation now accounts for nearly 70 percent of all freshwater use for human consumption. Efficient management of our shared natural resources and how we dispose of toxic waste and pollutants are important goals in achieving this goal. It is important to encourage industry, business and consumers to recycle and reduce pollution. A large portion of the world's population still consumes so little that their basic needs cannot be met.








Greenhouse gas emissions are more than 50 percent higher than in the 1990s. Global warming is causing long-term changes to our climate system, threatening irreversible consequences if we do not act. The average annual economic loss from climate-related disasters is in the hundreds of billions of dollars. Between 1998 and 2017, 1.3 million people died and 4.4 billion were injured. The goal is to mobilize $ 100 billion annually by 2020 to meet the needs of developing countries, both in adapting to climate change and investing in low-carbon development. These initiatives should go hand in hand with efforts to integrate disaster risk management, sustainable natural resource management and human security into national development strategies.







The world's oceans — their temperature, chemistry, currents, and life — control the global systems that make the earth habitable for mankind. This vital resource is also essential to balance the effects of climate change. More than three billion people depend on marine and coastal biodiversity for their livelihood. About 30 percent of man-made carbon dioxide is absorbed by the oceans, and we have seen a 26 percent increase in ocean acidity since the beginning of the Industrial Revolution. The vast majority of marine pollution comes from land-based sources, with an average of about 13,000 pieces of plastic per square kilometer, reaching dangerous levels. Improving conservation and sustainable use of marine resources through international law will also help mitigate some of the challenges facing our oceans.






Plant life provides 80 percent of human food, and we rely on agriculture as an important economic resource. Forests cover 30 percent of the earth's surface, provide important habitats for millions of species, and are important sources of clean air and water, as well as critical to combating climate change. Each year, 13 million hectares of forest are lost, and 3.6 billion hectares continue to be desertified due to the continuing depletion of dry land, which is unequally affecting the poorest communities. Wildlife trafficking not only erodes biodiversity, but also creates insecurity, incites conflict, and nurtures pollution. We must take urgent action to mitigate the loss of natural habitats and biodiversity that are part of our common heritage and support global food and water security, mitigation and adaptation to climate change, and peace and security.






Our world is becoming more and more divided. Some regions enjoy peace, security and prosperity, while others fall into endless cycles of conflict and violence. This is unavoidable and needs to be addressed. Armed violence and insecurity have a devastating effect on a country's development, economic growth, and often cause grievances that last for generations. Where sexual violence, crime, exploitation, and torture occur in conflict or lawlessness, countries must take steps to protect those at risk. The rule of law and the promotion of human rights are key to this process, as is reducing the flow of illegal weapons and strengthening the participation of developing countries in global governance.







Humanitarian crises caused by conflict or natural disasters continue to demand more financial resources and assistance. Many countries need formal development assistance to encourage growth and trade. The world is more interconnected than ever. Improving access to technology and knowledge is an important way to communicate and nurture innovation. Coordinating policies to assist developing countries in managing their debt, as well as promoting investment in the least developed countries, is essential for sustainable growth and development. The objectives are aimed at enhancing North-South and South-South cooperation by supporting national plans to achieve all objectives.
